Beat One

Opening Image

Page 1



Top

Establishes the tone, mood, type and scope of the story. Introduces the reader to the genre, world, time frame, age demographic, and tone. Also important for introducing the hero before the events of the story. The opening image creates a very clear "before" picture, so as the story unfolds, the viewer has context grounded for the after. At the end of thhe story, the opening image also provides contrast to Beat Fifteen: Final Image, or the "after."

The opening image:

  • Introduces of the hero
  • Define's a clear "before"
  • Sets up the setting, tone, and mood

Beat Two

Theme Stated

Page 5



Top

The "theme stated" introduces the viewer to the overall "why" of the story. The thematic premise is usually introduced by a secondary character. Focus on the "question" that the story is trying to answer. A good "theme stated" will have someone casually ask something like, "what is the point of all this if you can't make any money?" That draws the viewer's mind to the question, which the events of the story will later answer.

Theme stated:

  • Introduces the thematic premise
  • Is usually stated by a supporting character
  • Asks "the question" that the story aims to answer

Beat Three

Set-up

Pages 1-10



Top

Set-up completes the "Before" picture. All of the characters in the A Story must be introduced. The A-Story is the main plotline, the biggest picture answer to "What happens." The audience needs a clear understanding of what the characters feel is missing, and what their flaws are. Focus on primal desires - audiences relate to needs like love, acceptance, revenge, and anger. The rest of the story fixes what is missing in their lives. Tics will explode, create conflict, and be resolved by the end of the screenplay. Think of the set-up as the "Before," which should be mirrored in Beat Fourteen: Finale.

The set-up:

  • Develops before picture
  • Introduce all characters in the A Story
  • Identify what the characters want
  • Identify the characters' flaws

Beat Four

Catalyst

Page 22



Top

The catalyst, also known as the "inciting event," is what forces the character into action. Must disrupt the world created in Beat Three: Set-up. The catalyst works best if it's the result of a choice made by the character that explodes due to a flaw. Some catalysts are passive (a knock on the door, an unexpected letter) - but the main character must be presented with a choice.

The catalyst:

  • Forces the character to make a choice
  • Transitions from "Set-up" to "Debate"
  • The "old world" can't be an option

Beat Five

Debate

Pages 12-25



Top

The main character must struggle with a choice. The question isn't always "what do I do next." Sometimes, the debate centers around "can I do what I need to do?" or "how do I do it?" The internal struggle provides a break between the dramatic Catalyst and the equally dramatic events of Break Into Two

The debate:

  • Character struggles about what to do as a result of the catalyst
  • The choice can be what to do, how to do it, or uncertainty whether it can be done

Beat Six

Break Into Two

Page 25



Top

"Break Into Two" is the final departure from the "Old World." The hero must decide to go, and that decision must be included in a big break. Something "big" needs to happen at this point, in order for the audience to understand where in the story they are. The focus shifts from the "before" picture to the wild and uncertain transition in the middle of the story.

Break Into Two:

  • Hero must make a decision
  • The event must be "big"

Beat Seven

B-Story

Page 30



Top

If you consider your story's log-line the A-Story, the B-Story is a secondary story that runs alongside it. It carries the theme of the A-Story, but is new in scope. Often times, there are new characters, who are the antithesis of the old ones. Also often used for a love-story, or unlikely friendship. The purpose of the B-Story is to provide some relief from the A-Story, while running in parallel.

The B-Story:

  • Retains the theme of the A-Story
  • Often introduces new characters
  • Provides the audience some relief from the A-Story

Beat Eight

Fun and Games

Pages 30-55



Top

This is the meat of the story, in that it's the "promise of the premise." It explores the conflict inherent in the story. Breaks from the stakes that the A-Story set up, and instead explores ideas.

Fun and Games:

  • Delivers "the promise of the premise"
  • Explores conflicts and ideas

Beat Nine

Midpoint

Page 55



Top

The Midpoint of the screenplay provides eiher a false high or a false low point for the story. For example, characters win a battle against the enemy, but they redouble later. The story shifts significantly - almost like two different ideas. However, both ideas are in line with the A-Story. Raises the stakes, and serves as the inverse of All is Lost. Also functions as the literal halfway point of the story.

The midpoint:

  • Serves as a false high or low
  • Cuts the story in half
  • Each half explores a different area of the A-Story

Beat Ten

Bad Guys Close In

Pages 55-75



Top

The antagonists regroup after the "Midpoint." Conflict between the hero and their ensemble dissovlves the group, making them vulnerable. This is the transition from the Midpoint and into All is Lost, where the false high or low set up in the Midpoint is beginning to reverse.

Bad guys close in:

  • Reverses the Midpoint into All is Lost
  • Antagonists make a comeback from the Midpoint
  • Character conflict weakens the protagonists

Beat Eleven

All is Lost

Page 75



Top

Often labelled "the false defeat," can be summarized by the phrase "It's always darkest before the dawn." Previous decisions made by characters result in serious consequences. Blake Snyder recommends including a "whiff of death" at this point. The whiff of death can be the death of a character, a character contemplating suicide, or a dead plant. It serves to remind the audience of the stakes. Functions as the inverse of the Midpoint.

All is lost:

  • Stems from consequences from previous choices
  • Provides the "low point" for the heros struggle
  • Reminds the audience of death to reinforce the stakes

Beat Twelve

Dark Night of the Soul

Pages 75-85



Top

Similar to the Debate beat, it serves as a point for the character to struggle with the events in All is Lost. This is the point before the hero reaches down deep and makes the change required to overcome to conflict. The hero is frustrated, torn with regret, self-pity, hopeless, and clueless.

Dark night of the soul:

  • A direct response to All is Lost
  • The point before the hero understands how to resolve the conflict

Beat Thirteen

Break Into Three

Page 85



Top

This serves as the entry-point to the third act. Both the A and B story converge in order to provide the "eureka" solution for the hero. They solve the problem based on discussions of the theme in the B-Story, and are able to overcome their flaw and solve the problem.

Break into three:

  • Convergence of the A and B-story
  • Character overcomes their flaw based on discussions in the B-Story
  • Signals the Third Act

Beat Fourteen

Finale

Pages 85-110



Top

The hero masters their flaw and defeats the enemy. The old world yeilds to the new one. The bad guys are dispatched in ascending order, ending with the Big Bad. The problem must be completely solved in order for the new world to begin. Must provide the audience with emotional satisfaction, that the hero was able to overcome. Contrast to Setup

  • Hero masters their flaw
  • Enemies are completely defeated
  • The new world begins

Beat Fifteen

Final Image

Page 110



Top

A direct mirror of the Opening Image. It's best thought of as the "after" shot, compared with the Opening Image's before. Should show a distinct parallel, as well as a distinct change.

The final image:

  • Mirrors opening image
  • Shows what changed
  • Draws direct parallels between them.
