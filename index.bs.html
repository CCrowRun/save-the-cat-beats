Beat One

Opening Image

Page 1



Top

Establishes the tone, mood, type and scope of the story. Introduces the reader to the genre, world, time frame, age demographic, and tone. Also important for introducing the hero before the events of the story. The opening image creates a very clear "before" picture, so as the story unfolds, the viewer has context grounded for the after. At the end of thhe story, the opening image also provides contrast to Beat Fifteen: Final Image, or the "after."

The opening image:

  • Introduces of the hero
  • Define's a clear "before"
  • Sets up the setting, tone, and mood

Beat Two

Theme Stated

Page 5



Top

The "theme stated" introduces the viewer to the overall "why" of the story. The thematic premise is usually introduced by a secondary character. Focus on the "question" that the story is trying to answer. A good "theme stated" will have someone casually ask something like, "what is the point of all this if you can't make any money?" That draws the viewer's mind to the question, which the events of the story will later answer.

Theme stated:

  • Introduces the thematic premise
  • Is usually stated by a supporting character
  • Asks "the question" that the story aims to answer

Beat Three

Set-up

Pages 1-10



Top

Set-up completes the "Before" picture. All of the characters in the A Story must be introduced. The A-Story is the main plotline, the biggest picture answer to "What happens." The audience needs a clear understanding of what the characters feel is missing, and what their flaws are. Focus on primal desires - audiences relate to needs like love, acceptance, revenge, and anger. The rest of the story fixes what is missing in their lives. Tics will explode, create conflict, and be resolved by the end of the screenplay. Think of the set-up as the "Before," which should be mirrored in Beat Fourteen: Finale.

The set-up:

  • Develops before picture
  • Introduce all characters in the A Story
  • Identify what the characters want
  • Identify the characters' flaws

Beat Four

Catalyst

Page 22



Top

The catalyst, also known as the "inciting event," is what forces the character into action. Must disrupt the world created in Beat Three: Set-up. The catalyst works best if it's the result of a choice made by the character that explodes due to a flaw. Some catalysts are passive (a knock on the door, an unexpected letter) - but the main character must be presented with a choice.

The catalyst:

  • Forces the character to make a choice
  • Transitions from "Set-up" to "Debate"
  • The "old world" can't be an option

Beat Five

Debate

Pages 12-25



Top

The main character must struggle with a choice. The question isn't always "what do I do next." Sometimes, the debate centers around "can I do what I need to do?" or "how do I do it?" The internal struggle provides a break between the dramatic Catalyst and the equally dramatic events of Beat Six: Break Into Two

The debate:

  • Character struggles about what to do as a result of the catalyst
  • The choice can be what to do, how to do it, or uncertainty whether it can be done

Beat Six

Break Into Two

Page 25



Top

"Break Into Two" is the final departure from the "Old World." The hero must decide to go, and that decision must be included in a big break. Something "big" needs to happen at this point, in order for the audience to understand where in the story they are. The focus shifts from the "before" picture to the wild and uncertain transition in the middle of the story.

Break Into Two:

  • Hero must make a decision
  • The event must be "big"

Beat Seven

B-Story

Page 30



Top

If you consider your story's log-line the A-Story, the B-Story is a secondary story that runs alongside it. It carries the theme of the A-Story, but is new in scope. Often times, there are new characters, who are the antithesis of the old ones. Also often used for a love-story, or unlikely friendship. The purpose of the B-Story is to provide some relief from the A-Story, while running in parallel.

The B-Story:

  • Retains the theme of the A-Story
  • Often introduces new characters
  • Provides the audience some relief from the A-Story

Beat Eight

Fun and Games

Pages 30-55



Top

This is the meat of the story, in that it's the "promise of the premise." It explores the conflict inherent in the story. Breaks from the stakes that the A-Story set up, and instead explores ideas.

Fun and Games:

  • Delivers "the promise of the premise"
  • Explores conflicts and ideas
